Comparing Two Output Distributions

Study Overview

When you change a prompt, the distribution of LLM outputs changes too. A central question is: What changed between prompt A and prompt B? Answering this requires comparing two distributions—looking at diversity, typical vs. rare outputs, and what the two sets of outputs share or don't share.

In this study, you'll use a visualization tool to compare outputs from two different prompts. We're interested in how people reason about:

  • Distribution-level questions: Which prompt has higher diversity? What would you call an "average" output for each? What are rare or unusual outputs for each?
  • Overlap and difference: What are common elements (e.g., phrases, structure) in both sets of outputs? What elements differ between the two (e.g., phrasing, length, tone)?
  • Specific tasks with ground truth: Some questions have a verifiable answer (e.g., "What is the most common monster name in the outputs for each prompt?"). You may be asked to answer these and we can compare your answers to the data.

Your task is to explore the interface, compare the two distributions, and answer questions about how they differ. There are no trick questions—we want to see how the visualization supports this kind of comparison.

Task 1

In this task, you'll compare two distributions of outputs—one from prompt A and one from prompt B. Your goal is to reason about what changed between the two prompts by exploring both sets of outputs in the visualization.

As you explore, consider:

  • Diversity: Which prompt produces higher diversity in its outputs?
  • Typical vs. rare: What would you describe as an "average" or representative output for each? What are rare or unusual outputs for each?
  • Common elements: What phrases, patterns, or structural elements appear in both sets of outputs?
  • Different elements: What differs between the two sets (e.g., phrasing, length, tone, content)?
  • Specific questions: You may be asked concrete questions with a right answer (e.g., the most common name or phrase in the outputs for each prompt). Use the tool to find and report these when asked.

Use the interface to select and compare both prompts. There are no right or wrong answers for the qualitative comparisons; for specific factual questions, we're interested in whether the tool helps you find the correct answer.
